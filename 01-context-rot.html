Context rot, and why fresh windows matter

Every long-context model degrades as you fill it. The U-shape you've been hearing about is real, it has a name, and it's the mechanism behind every pattern in the rest of this guide — subagents, Ralph loops, plan-then-implement, all of it.

The U-curve

Liu et al.'s 2023 paper "Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts" ran multi-document question-answering across GPT-3.5, Claude 1.3, and others. They placed the relevant document at every position in the context and measured accuracy. The result was a U: information at the start did fine, information at the end did fine, information in the middle was forgotten.

It is not subtle. The accuracy gap between "start" and "middle" positions was 20+ points on GPT-3.5-Turbo at 20 documents. And it appeared in models explicitly trained for long contexts — the training didn't fix it.

It is not a 2023 problem

The 2025 Chroma technical report extended the test across 18 frontier models including GPT-4.1, Claude 4, Gemini 2.5, and Qwen3. Every model except one degraded as input length grew — not only near the limit, but at every increment. RULER benchmarks show GPT-4-1106 dropping from 96.6 at 4K tokens to 81.2 at 128K. Llama 3.1-70B drops 30 points over the same range. Gemini 2.5 Flash is the outlier, near-flat to its context limit.

the practical claim

You cannot trust "the model has a 200K context window" to mean "the model uses 200K tokens well." It almost never does. Performance at 30% capacity is usually noticeably better than at 70%, even when nothing technically broke.

Why this is the foundation of orchestration

Every pattern that gets celebrated in agent design is, mechanically, a context-management trick. Here is the same idea told three ways:

Subagents

A subagent runs in its own fresh context window. The instructions and tools start at the top, where attention works. The subagent returns a 500-token summary to the parent — which never had to pay attention to the 50K tokens of files the subagent read.

Ralph loop

Geoffrey Huntley's bash while loop re-invokes the agent with the same prompt every iteration. The whole point: each iteration starts at the top of a fresh window. State lives in files; the model rediscovers it. "It essentially mallocs arrays repeatedly."

Plan, then implement

A plan-mode pass produces a short artifact — a paragraph of plan, not 50K of code-reading. The implementer reads the plan at the top of its window, plus only the files it actually edits. The exploration cost is paid once and compressed.

Compact / clear

The blunt instrument. /compact in Claude Code summarizes the running session in place. /clear wipes it. Both buy you back a fresh top-of-window — at the cost of losing detail the summary can't preserve.

The "Dumb Zone"

Huntley's name for what happens past about 60–70% of a model's nominal context: the responses get vaguer, instructions earlier in the session are dropped, and the model starts confabulating about state it can't see. The numbers vary by model. The phenomenon does not.

Two operational consequences:

  1. Stop measuring sessions by elapsed time. Measure by context fill. Most agent UIs now show this. When you're past 60%, you are not getting your money's worth from the next turn — and you are unlikely to notice in real time, because the bad output looks like the good output.
  2. Don't put valuable instructions in the middle. Things you need the model to obey go in the system prompt or in AGENTS.md (loaded at the very top), not in the eighth conversational turn. By turn fifteen, that instruction is in the dead zone.

What this means for the rest of this guide

Three things are about to keep recurring:

  • Persistent files beat session memory. If something has to be true every turn, write it down. AGENTS.md, SPEC.md, PLAN.md, docs/notes/. The agent rediscovers them; that's a feature, not friction.
  • Fresh windows beat long sessions. Two thirty-thousand-token windows almost always outperform one sixty-thousand-token window. This is the actual reason multi-agent works when it works.
  • Verifiers replace memory. If the test suite passes, the agent doesn't need to remember the bug. The test is the memory.
